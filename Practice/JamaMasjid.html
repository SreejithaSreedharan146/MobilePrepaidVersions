About

The Jama Masjid, officially known as Masjid-i-Jehan-Numa, stands as one of India's largest and most renowned mosques. Commissioned by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan between 1644 and 1656, it served as the imperial mosque of the Mughal emperors until the end of the empire in 1857. Located in Old Delhi, opposite the Red Fort, it remains a significant symbol of Islamic heritage in India.

Address

Architect

The Jama Masjid in Delhi, commissioned by Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan and completed in 1656, is one of India's largest mosques. While Shah Jahan was the patron, the specific architect's name is not documented in historical records.

Visitors

The mosque's vast courtyard can accommodate up to 25,000 worshippers. However, exact annual visitor statistics are not readily available. Given its historical and architectural significance, it remains a major attraction for both worshippers and tourists throughout the year.
